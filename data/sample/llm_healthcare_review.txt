Large Language Models in Healthcare: A Comprehensive Review

1. Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as transformative tools in healthcare, offering
unprecedented capabilities in clinical decision support, medical documentation, and patient
engagement. This review examines the current state of LLM applications in healthcare as of 2024.

2. Clinical Decision Support

LLMs such as GPT-4, Med-PaLM 2 (developed by Google), and Claude have demonstrated remarkable
performance on medical licensing examinations. Med-PaLM 2 achieved an 85.4% score on the
United States Medical Licensing Examination (USMLE) Step 2 CK, surpassing the passing threshold
of 60%. GPT-4 scored approximately 86.7% on USMLE Step 1, marking a significant improvement
over GPT-3.5's 58.4%.

Key applications in clinical decision support include:
- Differential diagnosis generation
- Drug interaction checking
- Treatment recommendation systems
- Clinical trial matching

3. Medical Documentation and NLP

Electronic Health Records (EHR) documentation consumes approximately 49% of physician time.
LLMs are being deployed to:
- Generate clinical notes from patient encounters (ambient clinical documentation)
- Summarize lengthy medical records
- Extract structured data from unstructured clinical text
- Translate medical jargon into patient-friendly language

Notable implementations:
- Epic Systems integrated GPT-4 for generating patient message responses
- Nuance DAX Copilot (Microsoft) uses ambient AI for clinical documentation
- Amazon HealthScribe provides automated clinical note generation

4. Drug Discovery and Research

LLMs accelerate drug discovery through:
- Molecular property prediction
- Drug-target interaction modeling
- Clinical trial protocol optimization
- Literature mining for drug repurposing candidates

BioGPT, developed by Microsoft Research, was specifically trained on 15 million PubMed
abstracts and demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on biomedical text mining tasks.

5. Challenges and Risks

5.1 Hallucination Risk
LLMs can generate plausible but factually incorrect medical information. Studies have shown
hallucination rates of 5-15% in medical question-answering tasks, which is unacceptable in
clinical settings where accuracy is critical.

5.2 Bias and Fairness
Training data biases can lead to disparate performance across demographic groups. Research
has shown that LLMs may provide less accurate responses for conditions that disproportionately
affect underrepresented populations.

5.3 Data Privacy
HIPAA compliance remains a significant concern when processing patient data through LLMs.
De-identification must be robust, and data processing agreements with LLM providers must be
carefully structured.

5.4 Regulatory Framework
The FDA has approved over 950 AI/ML-enabled medical devices as of 2024, but regulatory
frameworks for LLM-based clinical tools are still evolving. The EU AI Act classifies medical
AI systems as "high-risk," requiring conformity assessments.

7. Future Directions

The healthcare AI market is projected to reach $187.95 billion by 2030, growing at a CAGR
of 37%. Key trends include:
- Multimodal models integrating text, imaging, and genomic data
- Federated learning for privacy-preserving model training
- Specialized medical foundation models
- Real-time clinical decision support at the point of care
- Integration with wearable health devices and IoT sensors

8. Conclusion

LLMs represent a paradigm shift in healthcare AI. While challenges remain in accuracy,
bias, privacy, and regulation, the potential to improve patient outcomes, reduce clinician
burnout, and accelerate medical research is substantial. Responsible deployment with robust
validation frameworks is essential for realizing these benefits.
